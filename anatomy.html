Heart & Blood Vessels

Anatomy of the
Circulatory System

An in-depth exploration of the heart's chambers, valves, blood vessels, and the components of blood that sustain life.

Part I

Chambers & Structures of the Heart

The human heart is divided into four chambers and contains specialized valves that ensure blood flows in only one direction.

🫀
Upper-Right Chamber

Right Atrium

The right atrium is the upper-right chamber of the heart. It receives deoxygenated blood returning from the body through two large veins: the superior vena cava (from the upper body) and the inferior vena cava (from the lower body). It also receives blood from the coronary sinus, which drains the heart's own blood supply.

Once filled, the right atrium contracts and pumps blood through the tricuspid valve into the right ventricle below it.

💓
Lower-Right Chamber

Right Ventricle

The right ventricle is the lower-right chamber. It receives deoxygenated blood from the right atrium and pumps it out through the pulmonary valve into the pulmonary artery, which carries it to the lungs for oxygenation.

The right ventricle has a thinner muscular wall than the left ventricle, since it only needs to pump blood the short distance to the lungs rather than throughout the entire body.

🩺
Upper-Left Chamber

Left Atrium

The left atrium receives freshly oxygenated blood returning from the lungs via the four pulmonary veins — the only veins in the body that carry oxygen-rich blood. It acts as a reservoir for oxygenated blood before passing it to the left ventricle.

The left atrium contracts to push blood through the mitral (bicuspid) valve into the powerful left ventricle. It is separated from the right atrium by the interatrial septum.

❤️‍🔥
Lower-Left Chamber — Main Pump

Left Ventricle

The left ventricle is the most muscular chamber of the heart and functions as the primary pump for systemic circulation. Its walls are approximately three times thicker than those of the right ventricle, enabling it to generate the high pressure needed to push blood throughout the entire body.

With each contraction (systole), the left ventricle forces blood through the aortic valve into the aorta, propelling it to all organs and tissues.

🔴
Great Artery

The Aorta

The aorta is the largest and most important artery in the human body, roughly 30 cm (12 inches) long and 2.5 cm (1 inch) in diameter. It originates at the left ventricle, arches upward and backward, then descends through the chest and abdomen to supply the entire systemic circulation.

It branches into major arteries including the coronary arteries (supplying the heart), carotid arteries (supplying the brain), and many others. It withstands enormous pressure with each heartbeat.

🚪
One-Way Gates

Heart Valves

The heart contains four valves that function like one-way doors, ensuring blood flows in only one direction and preventing backflow:

• Tricuspid Valve — between right atrium and right ventricle
• Pulmonary Valve — between right ventricle and pulmonary artery
• Mitral Valve — between left atrium and left ventricle
• Aortic Valve — between left ventricle and aorta

The familiar "lub-dub" heartbeat sound is the closing of these valves.

🧱
Internal Divider

The Septum

The septum is the thick muscular wall that divides the heart into left and right sides, keeping oxygenated and deoxygenated blood completely separate. There are two parts: the interatrial septum (between the atria) and the interventricular septum (between the ventricles).

A hole in the septum is known as a septal defect — one of the most common congenital heart conditions in newborns, allowing blood to mix between chambers.

Part II

The Blood Vessels

A vast network of tubes totaling over 60,000 miles in length, categorized into three main types based on structure and function.

🔴

Arteries

Arteries carry oxygenated blood away from the heart under high pressure. They have thick, muscular, elastic walls that expand and recoil with each heartbeat. The pulmonary arteries are the exception, carrying deoxygenated blood to the lungs. Arteries branch into smaller arterioles before reaching capillaries.

🔵

Veins

Veins carry deoxygenated blood back to the heart under lower pressure. They have thinner walls and contain one-way valves that prevent backflow. Skeletal muscle contractions and breathing help push blood through veins against gravity. They begin as tiny venules that merge into larger veins.

🔬

Capillaries

Capillaries are microscopic vessels just one cell thick (5–10 micrometers), forming dense networks called capillary beds. They are the only vessels where actual gas, nutrient, and waste exchange occurs between blood and body tissues. Their thin walls allow oxygen and glucose to diffuse out while CO₂ and waste diffuse in.

Part III

Components of Blood

Blood is a specialized connective tissue composed of four distinct components, each serving essential physiological roles.

Blood Composition by Volume

Plasma55%
Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)43%
White Blood Cells (Leukocytes)1%
Platelets (Thrombocytes)1%
🔴

Red Blood Cells

Erythrocytes are disc-shaped cells packed with hemoglobin, an iron-containing protein that binds oxygen. They lack a nucleus, maximizing space for hemoglobin. The average adult has about 25 trillion RBCs. Each lives for about 120 days before being recycled in the spleen.

🛡️

White Blood Cells

Leukocytes form the cellular arm of the immune system. They identify and destroy pathogens, foreign particles, and cancerous cells. Types include neutrophils, lymphocytes (T cells and B cells), monocytes, eosinophils, and basophils. Though far fewer in number than RBCs, they are vital defenders.

🩹

Platelets

Thrombocytes are tiny cell fragments produced from large cells called megakaryocytes in bone marrow. When a blood vessel is injured, platelets rush to the site and clump together to form a plug, initiating the clotting (coagulation) cascade that stops bleeding. Normal count: 150,000–400,000 per μL.

💛

Plasma

Plasma is the pale yellow liquid that makes up about 55% of blood volume, consisting of ~90% water. It carries dissolved nutrients (glucose, amino acids, lipids), hormones, clotting factors, antibodies, and waste products (urea, CO₂). It also maintains blood pressure and body temperature balance.
